Scenario: Fire Marshal Adds Equipment Requirement

Your team is midway through execution when the unexpected happens. During the facility inspection, the fire marshal informs you that a new Double Check Detector Assembly (DCDA) is now required for backflow prevention of the sprinkler system. This component is essential to obtain final approval for your sprinkler system - but it was not included in the original scope or budget due to it being a new permit requirement.

The DCDA will:

  • Add approximately $10,000 to your project cost (DCDA, installation materials, labor, and re-inspection)
  • Add approximately 15 days to order and install

The fire marshal also informs you of an alternative option: Installing a dry chemical suppression system instead of a sprinkler system. This would be a more cost-effective solution, but may come with a longer schedule impact. Switching to the dry chemical system will:

  • Add approximately $6,000 to your project cost (Dry chemical suppression system, installation materials, labor, re-inspection)
  • Add approximately 30 days to order and install
  • Minus the originally budgeted sprinkler installation costs

Objective

Complete a Change Request Form that includes:

  • A brief description of the issue and trigger event
  • An analysis of scope, schedule, and cost impact
  • Options considered
  • Potential future risks and benefits
  • Your team's recommended action

Scenario: Paint Booth Delay ‚Äì Vendor Offers Alternate Model

Your team is preparing for the paint booth installation when you receive an unexpected update from your vendor. Due to logistics and supply chain issues, the originally ordered booth model will be delayed by an additional 30 days, beyond the original 30-day estimate. This delay puts pressure on your project timeline and risks pushing downstream activities into conflict with permit deadlines and resource availability.

The vendor offers an alternative:

  • A different booth model with similar specifications, no additional facility modifications required
  • Available in 21 days - 9 days earlier than the original unit
  • $10,000 more expensive
  • Slightly more energy-efficient, which could yield long-term savings

Your team must now evaluate whether to accept the delay or proceed with the alternative. This scenario requires careful analysis of cost vs. time trade-offs, potential operational benefits, and how the decision affects the overall project baseline. In summary:

Option 1: Accept the delay and proceed with the original model

  • Estimated cost impact: +$0
  • Schedule impact: +30 days

Option 2: Proceed with the alternative model

  • Estimated cost impact: +$10,000
  • Schedule impact: -9 days
